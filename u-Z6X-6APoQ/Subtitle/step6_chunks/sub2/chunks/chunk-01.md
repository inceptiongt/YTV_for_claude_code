[59] Before it became a landmark, the Bay Bridge was a big gamble, a hugely ambitious attempt to force a permanent road across one of Maryland's biggest obstacles.
[60] Fairies could not keep pace forever.
[61] And while their numbers sound staggering, they were only able to move about 100,000 vehicles annually across the bay.
[62] For a backdrop to that number, the first year the bridge was opened in 1952, it hosted nearly 1.1 million vehicles.
[63] So deciding to build a bridge was one thing. Figuring out what kind of bridge could actually work here quite another.
[64] One of the first things people notice about the Bay Bridge, at least the original span that was built in 52, is that it almost looks like somebody tried to build a sample kit of every bridge type in America.
[65] But that mixed look has a purpose.
[66] No single design could do the whole job.
[67] Near the Western Shore, the bridge had to rise enough to clear the main shipping channel.
[68] Other parts of the Bay Bridge didn't need that kind of structure, so the crossing changed with the bay itself.
[69] Suspension spans carried the road over the main channel.
[70] Canol lever and truss sections handled the other navigational openings.
[71] Lower beam and girder approaches took over where the water was shallower and the demands of shipping traffic were different.
[72] That mixed design is worth noticing because it helps explain why the bridge still feels so unusual to drive even now.
[73] Even the bridge's big curve comes from function, not aesthetics.
[74] The main span had to cross the shipping channel at a right angle and that requirement shaped the drive itself.
[75] But solving the crossing on paper was only part of the challenge.
[76] Building it out there was another problem entirely.
[77] The original crossing was designed by Baltimore's J Grinder Company with major steel components fabricated by Bethlehem Steel.
[78] More than 2,000 workers spent over six million man-hour on the project with steel sections carried out by barge and lifted into place by floating and fixed cranes.
[79] Some workers were even housed in a floating hotel during construction.
[80] It had to be built progressively farther out over open water.
[81] Under the conditions of a large and demanding marine job, while fitting the superructure was a major task, the harder fight was found below the water line.
[82] Crews were not working with convenient shallow bedrock.
[83] The Chesapeake only offered deep sediments, mud, and buried layers that made the bay floor far less cooperative than the finished bridge actually makes it look.
[84] The first job was to create a stable pier foundation.
[85] Enormous steel forums called quesons were built first, then sunk to the bottom of the bay.
