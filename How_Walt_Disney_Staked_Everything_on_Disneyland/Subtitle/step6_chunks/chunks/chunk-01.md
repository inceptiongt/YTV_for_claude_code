# Intro

[1] In the summer of 1955, Walt Disney had a problem.
[2] He was a few weeks off opening his most audacious venture yet, and he had a decision to make.
[3] >> Should he install flushing toilets or drinking fountains? His plumbers had gone on strike, and there wasn't enough time to do both.
[4] This was the last thing he needed.
[5] He was heavily in debt and had decided to stake not just his reputation but his personal fortune on building an amusement park, something which was normally free to enter. But he
[6] needn't have worried.
[7] By combining technical wizardry with the magic of the big screen, Disney had hit upon his most consequential innovation yet, one that would transform his dwindling kingdom into a global empire.
[8] As Disneyland celebrates its 70th birthday, this is the story of how Walt Disney combined silvercreen magic with the miracle of construction to build the happiest place on earth.

# In a rut

[9] At the end of World War II, Walt Disney was in a bind.
[10] His movie studio had made it through the war making propaganda films, but he was now heavily in debt, and it was beginning to seem like the glory days of Disney were in the past.
[11] But the man behind the world's most famous mouse wasn't done yet.
[12] A chance visit to a railroad fair in Chicago in 1948 sparked an interest in fairground rides. Amusement parks and fairs were nothing new at the time,
[13] but for Disney, they lacked a certain magic.
[14] Usually, they were designed around the rides with some attention given to scene setting and decoration, but nothing resembling any kind of creative vision.
[15] Disney wanted to create something new, a place with rides and attractions, but more than that, a place that would really tell a story.
[16] He wanted to project an image of an idyllic world, one of innocence and wholesomeness, where children could let their imaginations run free.
[17] To get his idea off the ground, Disney called in art director Herb Ryman. And over the course of a frantic weekend in September 1953, they developed the initial concept for Disneyland.

# Inspiration strikes

[18] What they created was something radically different to anything that had come before. The first major difference came in the layouts.
[19] When researching other parks, Disney found that one of their weaknesses was they often lacked a coherent plan,
[20] making them pretty confusing to navigate.
[21] If he were to create the happiest place on earth, it would have to be simple to get around.
[22] And to do that, Disney had to meticulously design the flow of people as well as the attractions. And that all began at the entrance.
[23] At the time, amusement parks in the US were typically free to enter and as such had multiple entrances. Disneyland,
[24] however, would have just one. By restricting access to the park in this way, Disney was able to create a tightly curated experience from the moment guests stepped foot in his park.

# The plan

[25] Visitors would be greeted by an embankments on which sat a station for a miniature railway that ran the perimeter of the entire park.
[26] This would also conceal the park's first illusion. The embankment doubling as a physical barrier to prevent the sights and sounds of the outside world from getting in.
[27] From there, they would enter the park via a tunnel which Disney called the stage curtains, a small plaque marking the point at which guests left the real world behind and entered Disney's imagination.
[28] First stop after entering was Main Street USA, an idolized version of the American High Street, heavily based on Disney's hometown of Marceline in Missouri.
[29] From there, the park would make use of eye-catching visual elements, which Disney would coin as weenies, to pull people around the park.
[30] The grandest of these would, of course, be a fantasy castle, which would draw visitors towards a central plaza, the hub from which every land, ride, and attraction would radiate out.
[31] As well as making navigation easier, it would create a point at which people could meet and rest. Branching off from this were a series of themed areas.
[32] Other parks had often included novelty buildings to give a sense of the exotic, but these would be standalone features rather than part of a coherent theme.
[33] What Disney planned was total immersion.
[34] Just like the park itself, each land would be visually cut off from anything that might spoil the illusion.
[35] With the concept sketches complete, Disney sent his brother Roy off to New York to raise money for the build.

# Work begins

[36] But how do you turn something this new and unique into a reality? Disney had no experience in building anything on this scale, let alone something that broke with this much precedent.
[37] He figured relying purely on imagination would get him nowhere.
[38] But engineering on its own wasn't very entertaining.
[39] He needed people who could combine artistic creativity with practical skills to create something unique. What he needed was an imagineer.
[40] Fortunately, Hollywood was full of them.
[41] He turned to set designers and artists in his own company, but also from a powerful rival.
[42] 20th Century Fox had a back lot four times the size of Disneyland. And it was filled with scenic artists used to building everything from city streets to steamboats.
[43] It was from here that Disney filled the ranks of his new army of imagineers.
[44] The design process started with a pitch which was then worked up into storyboards and further on into models of increasing sizes.
[45] The whole process was fluid with constant revisions and alterations made throughout, including on some of the park's biggest structures.
[46] This is Nushmanstein Castle in southern Germany. It was used as the inspiration
[47] for the iconic Sleeping Beauty Castle which lays at the head of the park.

# Tricks of the trade

[48] It has a gate house, turrets, and palace, all of which provided a reference for Disney's version.
[49] On the front of the palace, it has a balcony which leads from the spectacular Singer's Hall.
[50] This detail was initially included in the plans, but in a sign of how fluid the development was, it was changed purely by chance.
[51] During the design phase, engineers had taken the model apart for cleaning, only to quickly reassemble it when Walt Disney came to visit.
[52] In the rush, they put the palace back on the wrong way round.
[53] Disney noticed the mistake, but actually preferred the way it looked and had the castle built as the model stood.
[54] The balcony still lives on today, but at the back of the castle.
[55] This level of improvisation didn't stop at the design stage.
[56] The Jungle Cruise has become one of the most popular rides of Disneyland and has since been replicated at other Disney parks. Designing a ride like this today requires months of planning and design work by huge teams of people.
[57] But back in 1954, the ride's layout was solely down to this man, legendary art director Harper Goff.
[58] With a bulldozer driver looking on, Goff took a stick and walked around the ride's plot, drawing a line in the soil.
[59] Once completed, he drew a parallel line a few meters away. The bulldozer followed, piling up the excavated soil on the embankments, and the layout of the jungle cruise was complete.
[60] Now, this might all sound a bit free jazz, but there was a lot at stake.
[61] If Disney didn't pull this off, he stood to lose everything.
[62] As Disney's ambitions for the park grew, so did the bill to pay for it all.
[63] By the time construction began, he'd taken out $17 million in loans, equivalent to over $600 million today.
[64] He even put his personal fortune on the line, selling properties and cashing in his life insurance to raise funds.
[65] In 1954, he had the financing in place. But with so much money at stake,
[66] he needed the park earning as soon as possible, and that meant opening the gates in the lucrative vacation season the following summer.
[67] On the 16th of July 1954, a year and a day before the grand opening, Disneyland broke ground.
