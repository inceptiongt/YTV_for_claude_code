# How it's built

[54] Let's take a look at that bridge.
[55] Two new concrete factories were created on the island to cast the enormous components for the structure.
[56] The bridge is a box girder design, meaning the spans are created by joining together a series of precast hollow structures.
[57] Inside these boxes are prestressed steel cables, which squeeze the different sections together.
[58] All of that sits on 48 piers, each carefully designed to minimize resistance to the tides and swells of water.
[59] The piers were cast here, near the western tip of the road. Each structure is made of two blocks.
[60] The base, 15 m tall, weighing 4,500 tons and featuring a 23-m wide foundation. On top of that, the pier head, measuring between 9 and 21 m.
[61] To transport these concrete giants to the work sites, a special piece of equipment was needed.
[62] Say hello to Zurit.
[63] While everything else for this project was sourced as close as possible, this was the exception.
[64] Constructed in Poland, this specially designed barge is the size of a football pitch and contains a concrete mixing facility and gantry capable of lifting the enormous pier sections.
[65] One at a time, foundations are sailed out. Once in position, Zurit's crew, for octopus, plants eight steel legs into the seabed to provide stability. The foundations are then precisely lowered into place.
[66] Once on the sea floor, jacks raise the structure up 40 cm, allowing concrete to be injected into sacks beneath the base plates. It took 4 years to install all 48 piers.
[67] Once in place, a beam launcher was used to install the box girders. Standing on a pier, it picks up deck pieces and installs them either side on a pier, two at a time to maintain balance.
[68] It takes a month to complete two spans, after which the launcher relocates to the next pier and repeats the process.
[69] In 2017, after 4 years of work, the Grand Chaloupe Viaduct was complete.
